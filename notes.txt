SIDEBAR CONTROLS (30 seconds)
-------------------------------
"On the left, I have three controls: a Year Range slider, a State filter, and a Drug Category
selector. These are the user's controls — no coding required to use them.

I'll leave states blank to show national data, and I'll keep the default drug categories which cover

KPI CARDS — TOP ROW (1 minute)
--------------------------------
"At the top, four summary cards give you the headline numbers immediately.

The first — Latest 12-Month Total. This is the provisional rolling death count from the CDC's most
recent release. I'll always note: these are provisional figures. Final counts from the National
Center for Health Statistics may differ slightly, but the provisional data is what policymakers are
working from right now.

The second — All-Time Peak. The app automatically finds the worst recorded 12-month period in the
dataset. You don't have to hunt for it.

Third — Synthetic Opioid Deaths and their share of total overdoses. This is the headline statistic.
Fentanyl now accounts for roughly 70% of all overdose deaths — up from under 15% in 2015. That
single number tells the entire story of the opioid crisis shift.

Fourth — States Reporting and the highest-burden state. This sets up the geographic tab."


TAB 1: NATIONAL TRENDS (2 minutes — main demo)
------------------------------------------------
"This is the heart of the dashboard. The line chart shows 12-month rolling overdose deaths over time,
broken out by drug category.

The first thing you notice is the inflection around 2016 to 2017. That is the moment fentanyl
entered the illicit drug supply at scale. Before that, the crisis was driven by prescription opioids
and heroin. After that, synthetic opioids took over completely.

See this yellow band? That is the COVID-19 period — March 2020 through mid-2021. The spike you see
is not a coincidence. Isolation, disrupted treatment access, and supply chain shifts all hit
simultaneously. 2020 recorded the largest single-year jump in overdose deaths in US history.

Watch this — I'll uncheck Heroin from the drug categories. [deselect Heroin] Now look at the
fentanyl line in isolation. Then I'll add Heroin back. [add back] You can see the inverse
relationship — as heroin declined, fentanyl rose almost exactly in its place. That is the
substitution effect, and it's one of the most important trends in modern public health.

The bar chart below shows year-over-year change. Recent years show a potential plateauing — that
is the most hopeful signal in this dataset."


TAB 2: STATE EXPLORER (1 minute)
----------------------------------
"This tab goes geographic. The choropleth map shows overdose deaths by state for whatever year is
selected in the dropdown. Move through the years and watch the crisis spread westward — what began
concentrated in Appalachia expanded to cover nearly every region of the country.

The ranking chart on the right shows the top 20 states by death count. West Virginia consistently
leads — not in absolute numbers, but when you account for population size, it has carried the
heaviest burden of any state for over a decade.

Select states in the sidebar filter, and a trend comparison chart appears at the bottom — letting
you track specific states side by side over the full time range."


TAB 3: DRUG BREAKDOWN (1 minute)
----------------------------------
"This tab shows the composition of the crisis, not just the scale.

The stacked area chart shows how the mix of substances has shifted over time. Heroin shrinks.
Fentanyl expands. Cocaine and methamphetamine grow on separate tracks, showing the crisis is no
longer just opioids — it is a broader polysubstance emergency.

The donut chart gives you the current year's drug share at a glance. Fentanyl's slice is the
most visually striking thing in the entire dashboard — and that is intentional.

The Opioid Shift chart in the bottom right shows the crossover point — the exact year Fentanyl
surpassed Heroin. For most audiences, this single chart is the one they remember."

KEY STATS (if asked)
---------------------
- Drug overdose deaths rose from ~52,000 in 2015 to over 110,000 in 2022
- Synthetic opioids (fentanyl) now account for ~70% of all overdose deaths
- Data covers all 50 states plus DC, reported monthly with a 6–12 month lag
- Source: CDC VSRR Provisional Drug Overdose Death Counts
- App fetches data via the Socrata Open Data API at data.cdc.gov — same infrastructure
  powering hundreds of government data portals
- 12-month rolling counts are used (not monthly snapshots) for statistical stability
- Cache TTL is 1 hour — data refreshes automatically on next load after expiry


COMMON QUESTIONS
-----------------
Q: Is this data real-time?
A: Provisional — CDC releases updates monthly. The app caches for 1 hour and fetches
   automatically. Final counts may differ slightly but provisional data is current.

Q: Can you add authentication?
A: Yes — Posit Connect integrates with LDAP, Active Directory, SAML, and OAuth.
   Lock it to internal users or make it public with one toggle in the admin UI.

Q: What about R users who don't know Python?
A: They don't need to. The R Shiny version of this demo is deployed from the same
   Connect server. Both teams work in their preferred language; Connect handles both.

Q: How does dependency management work?
A: Connect reads requirements.txt and builds an isolated environment per deployment.
   No dependency conflicts, no manual setup, no IT ticket required.
